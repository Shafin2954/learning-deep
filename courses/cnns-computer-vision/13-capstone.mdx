## The project

You will build a complete vision pipeline: implement convolution forward and backward passes
manually, train a custom CNN on an image classification task, and then construct either an
anchor-based detector head or a U-Net segmenter — evaluating with mAP or mIoU. The point is
not to beat Torchvision's pretrained models; it is to prove you can build and debug the
building blocks that make modern computer vision work.

## Hard constraints (no vibe coding)

- You MAY NOT import any pretrained vision backbone for the core task. Define your own
  architecture and train from scratch (or from random init).
- You MAY NOT use a library's built-in IoU, NMS, or mAP implementation — implement these
  yourself in NumPy.
- You MAY NOT use a detection or segmentation framework (Detectron2, MMDetection, etc.) for
  the core pipeline. Torchvision's `ops` module is off-limits for the core; it is allowed
  only for validation.
- You MAY use Torchvision `DataLoader`, `transforms`, and dataset classes.
- You MAY use PyTorch's `nn.Conv2d` for the training pipeline after you have implemented at
  least one conv forward + backward by hand.

## What it must contain (and the chapter each tests)

- **Manual convolution** (ch. 1–2): implement 2D convolution forward pass in NumPy; verify it
  matches `scipy.signal.correlate2d`. Implement the backward pass (gradient w.r.t. input and
  weight); verify with numerical gradients.
- **Custom CNN** (ch. 3–5): build a CNN in PyTorch with at least three conv-pool stages and a
  BatchNorm layer; train on CIFAR-10 or a similarly sized dataset to ≥ 80% test accuracy.
- **Data augmentation pipeline** (ch. 7): apply at least three augmentation types during
  training (random crop, horizontal flip, color jitter); show that augmentation improves
  validation accuracy over the unaugmented baseline.
- **IoU and NMS** (ch. 8): implement both in NumPy; test on at least five synthetic bounding
  box sets and verify outputs against Torchvision `ops.nms`.
- **Detection head or segmenter** (ch. 9–10): either (a) attach an anchor-based detection head
  to your CNN backbone and train it on a small object detection dataset (Pascal VOC subset or
  a toy dataset), reporting mAP@0.5; or (b) implement a U-Net with skip connections in
  PyTorch and train it on a binary segmentation task, reporting mIoU.
- **ViT comparison** (ch. 11): train or fine-tune a small ViT on the same classification
  dataset and compare accuracy, training time, and data efficiency vs your CNN.
- **Mixed precision** (DL Foundations, ch. 13): use `torch.cuda.amp.autocast` +
  `GradScaler` in your training loop; report the wall-clock speedup vs FP32.

## Acceptance criteria

- Manual convolution matches `scipy.signal.correlate2d` to floating-point tolerance on at
  least three kernel sizes (3×3, 5×5, 7×7) and at least two input sizes.
- Gradient check passes: max absolute error between analytic and numerical gradient < 1e-5.
- CNN reaches ≥ 80% test accuracy on CIFAR-10 (or equivalent dataset) within 30 epochs.
- IoU implementation matches Torchvision on 10 random box pairs to four decimal places.
- NMS implementation produces identical output order to Torchvision `ops.nms` (same threshold).
- Detection head reports mAP@0.5 ≥ 0.40 on the chosen dataset, or U-Net reports mIoU ≥ 0.70.
- A clear comparison table: CNN vs ViT — accuracy, parameters, epochs to convergence.
- Training loop uses mixed precision; timing logs show ≥ 1.3× speedup on GPU.

  effect on validation accuracy.
- [ ] I can implement IoU from scratch and explain the difference between IoU and GIoU.
- [ ] I can implement greedy NMS from scratch and explain what the IOU threshold controls.
- [ ] I can compute mAP@0.5 by hand (precision-recall curve → AP per class → mean).
- [ ] I can implement a U-Net encoder-decoder with skip connections and report mIoU on a
  segmentation task.

- [ ] I can compare CNN and ViT training dynamics and explain when ViT needs more data.
