## The project

You will build a working reverse-mode autograd engine and use it to implement a full MLP training pipeline — forward pass, backward pass, optimizers, and regularization — all without touching `torch.autograd` or `torch.nn`. At the end, your hand-rolled network trains on a real dataset and matches a PyTorch baseline within 2% accuracy. This is the proof that you understand what PyTorch is actually doing under the hood.

## Hard constraints (no vibe coding)

- **No `torch.autograd`** for any differentiation. You define `Value` (or `Tensor`) nodes with a manual `_backward` closure.
- **No `torch.nn`** for any layer. `nn.Linear`, `nn.ReLU`, `nn.BatchNorm`, `nn.Dropout` — all implemented by you.
- **No `torch.optim`** for SGD/Adam. Implement the update rules yourself.
- You may use `torch.Tensor` (or `numpy.ndarray`) as the raw array substrate — storage and matmul are fine; autograd is not.
- You may use `torch.utils.data.DataLoader` to load batches.
- You may use PyTorch only to run the baseline comparison, not inside your engine.

## What it must contain (and the chapter each tests)

- **Scalar or tensor autograd** with support for `+`, `*`, `@` (matmul), `relu`, `sigmoid`, `log`, and `sum` (ch. 3–4). Each op stores a `_backward` closure that propagates `grad` to its inputs.
- **Topological sort** for correct gradient accumulation order (ch. 3). A node visited from two paths must accumulate, not overwrite.
- **MLP class** with configurable depth and width, using your own Linear layers (ch. 1–2).
- **BatchNorm and Dropout** implemented by you, including train/eval mode switching (ch. 8–9).
- **SGD with momentum and Adam** implemented by you (ch. 6). Parameters are stored as your autograd nodes.
- **Training loop** with your engine: forward, loss, `.backward()`, optimizer step, `zero_grad` (ch. 10).
- **Gradient clipping** via your own `clip_grad_norm` (ch. 13).
- **Validation accuracy** tracked per epoch; best checkpoint saved as a dict of numpy arrays (ch. 10).
- **Comparison**: run the exact same architecture and hyperparameters in PyTorch `nn` and confirm your accuracy is within 2% (ch. 10).

## Acceptance criteria

- Your autograd passes a numerical gradient check on at least 3 different expression graphs (tolerance: absolute error < 1e-5).
- Your MLP trains to ≥ 80% on a linearly-separable synthetic dataset (20D, 1000 samples) within 50 epochs.
- Your Adam implementation matches `torch.optim.Adam` weight trajectories to within 1% relative error over 100 steps on a convex problem.
- Your BatchNorm forward output matches `torch.nn.BatchNorm1d` (eval mode) to within 1e-4 on a held-out batch.
- Your Dropout produces the correct expected value: `E[dropout(x)] ≈ x` (verify with 10,000 samples, tolerance 1e-2).
- Final val accuracy gap between your engine and PyTorch baseline ≤ 2%.
