## The project

Build a **statistical inference toolkit entirely from scratch** and use it to write a rigorous, caveated analysis of a real dataset. The toolkit must implement MLE, bootstrapping, hypothesis testing, multiple-testing correction, Bayesian updating, and probability calibration — all in pure NumPy and pandas, without relying on statsmodels or scipy.stats for the core logic (you may use them for numerical verification). The goal is to prove you understand the machinery behind the statistics functions you use daily, not just how to call them.

## Hard constraints (no shortcuts)

- **No statsmodels or scipy.stats for core implementations.** You may import them to verify your results (tolerances below), but every algorithm must first be implemented by hand.
- **All assumptions must be stated explicitly.** For each test, write one sentence: "This test assumes X; here is why I believe it holds (or doesn't) for this dataset."
- **No AI writes your statistical narrative.** The conclusions, caveats, and interpretation must be your own reasoning.
- **Pure NumPy + pandas** for all implementations. `scipy.optimize.minimize` is allowed for numerical MLE.

## What it must contain

### 1. MLE for three distributions (ch. 9)

Implement MLE for the Normal, Poisson, and Exponential distributions:
- Derive the analytic estimators and implement them.
- Also implement numerical MLE by minimizing the negative log-likelihood via `scipy.optimize.minimize`.
- Verify that analytic and numerical agree to 1e-4 absolute tolerance.
- Verify that your MLE matches `scipy.stats` MLE to 1e-4.

### 2. Bootstrap CI engine (ch. 10)

Implement two bootstrap confidence interval methods:
- **Percentile bootstrap:** resample with replacement $B=5000$ times, report the $\alpha/2$ and $1-\alpha/2$ percentiles.
- **BCa bootstrap:** implement bias-correction ($z_0$) and acceleration ($a$ via jackknife) and adjust the percentile indices accordingly.
- Demonstrate both on the median of an Exponential(1) sample ($n=60$); verify coverage via a simulation of 1,000 trials.

### 3. Permutation test for two-sample means (ch. 11)

Implement a permutation test:
- Under $H_0$, the group labels are exchangeable; compute the observed difference in means, then shuffle labels $B=5000$ times and count the fraction of shuffled differences as extreme as the observed.
- Apply it to a dataset where you know the true effect and a dataset where $H_0$ is true.
- Compare the p-value to `scipy.stats.ttest_ind`; they should be similar for moderate $n$.

### 4. BH false discovery rate procedure (ch. 12)

Implement the Benjamini-Hochberg procedure from scratch:
- Sort p-values, apply the BH threshold line $\frac{k}{m}\alpha$, find the largest $k$ that satisfies it.
- Simulate 1,000 tests (900 null, 100 true effects with Cohen's $d=0.6$, $n=25$) and verify that the empirical FDR is ≤ 0.05 at declared $\alpha=0.05$.
- Report both FDR and power (fraction of true effects recovered).

### 5. Conjugate Bayesian updater (ch. 16)

Implement sequential Bayesian updating for:
- **Beta-Binomial:** prior $\text{Beta}(\alpha_0, \beta_0)$; update after each Bernoulli observation; plot how the posterior mean and CI evolve across 50 coin flips.
- **Normal-Normal:** prior $N(\mu_0, \tau_0^2)$ with known observation variance $\sigma^2$; update after each batch of observations; show posterior mean converging to the true mean.

### 6. Calibration analysis (ch. 15)

Take any binary classifier on a real or simulated dataset (e.g., Random Forest, Naive Bayes):
- Plot the reliability diagram before and after calibration.
- Compute ECE and Brier score before and after Platt scaling and isotonic regression.
- Explain which calibration method works better and why.

### 7. Real-data statistical analysis (ch. 7–11, 14)

Choose a dataset with at least 3 numeric variables and one categorical variable. Perform:
- A normality check for each numeric variable (not an excuse to assume normality blindly).
- Appropriate correlation analysis (Pearson vs Spearman — justify the choice).
- At least one hypothesis test comparing two groups, with stated assumptions and caveats.
- Multiple-testing correction if more than one test is run.
- A one-paragraph conclusion that includes confidence levels and limitations.

## Acceptance criteria

- MLE estimates match `scipy.stats` to 1e-4 absolute tolerance for all three distributions.
- Bootstrap 95% percentile CI achieves empirical coverage ≥ 0.93 in a 1,000-trial simulation on Exp(1) medians with $n=60$.
- BH procedure empirical FDR ≤ 0.05 at declared $\alpha=0.05$ in the simulation.
- All Bayesian posteriors are proper distributions (integrate to 1; verified by checking that the normalizing constant matches the known conjugate form).
- Calibration ECE decreases after applying at least one calibration method.
- Every test in the real-data analysis has an explicit assumption statement and caveat.

- [ ] I can implement the BCa bootstrap including the bias-correction ($z_0$) and acceleration ($a$) adjustments, and explain what each corrects for.
- [ ] I can explain the difference between a p-value and a posterior probability, and why confusing them leads to incorrect conclusions.
- [ ] I can state the conditions under which BH controls FDR and explain why it has more power than Bonferroni correction.
- [ ] I can implement conjugate Bayesian updates for Beta-Binomial and Normal-Normal, and show numerically that the posterior mean is a precision-weighted average of the prior and data.
- [ ] I can calibrate a classifier's predicted probabilities using Platt scaling and isotonic regression, and explain when each is preferred.
- [ ] I can choose between Pearson and Spearman correlation for a given dataset and justify the choice in one sentence.
- [ ] I can interpret a confidence interval correctly (as a property of the procedure, not the specific interval) and avoid the common misinterpretation.
