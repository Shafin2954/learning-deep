## The project

Two parts, both **from scratch with no AI assistance** — the proof that you own tabular Kaggle
end to end.

**Part A** — Build a gradient boosting machine that matches LightGBM. Extend your ch. 6 GBM
into a real one and show it competes with the library on a real dataset.

**Part B** — Run a full competition pipeline on a public tabular dataset (a Playground Series
month, or any Kaggle tabular comp), applying the whole track: CV, features, models, ensemble,
metric optimization, submission.

If you can do both unaided, you're a complete tabular competitor.

## Hard constraints (no vibe coding)

- **No AI writes your code or makes your decisions.** Docs and these chapters only.
- **Part A is genuinely from scratch** — your own tree, your own boosting loop, your own
  regularization. You may use NumPy. You may *compare against* LightGBM but not call it for the
  core.
- **In Part B**, you may use the libraries (you built the engine in Part A; re-deriving it
  isn't the point), but the **pipeline is yours**: your own CV, your own target encoding, your
  own metric, your own ensembling/OOF discipline, your own post-processing.
- **Every decision traces to CV evidence**, not vibes.

## Part A — the from-scratch GBM (chapters it tests)

Extend ch. 6's gradient boosting with the real-library features (ch. 4–7):

1. A decision-tree learner with `max_depth` and `min_leaf` (ch. 4).
2. The boosting loop fitting trees to the negative gradient, with **learning\_rate** (ch. 6).
3. **Row subsampling** and **column subsampling** (stochastic boosting, ch. 5–6).
4. **Early stopping** on a validation set, keeping the best round (ch. 6, F6).
5. **Binary classification** via the log-loss gradient ($y - \sigma(F)$), so it does
   classification, not just regression (ch. 6 extension).
6. **Feature importance** by gain (ch. 7, 11).

**Acceptance:** on a real tabular dataset, your GBM's CV score is within a small margin of
LightGBM's with comparable settings (your ch. 6 prototype already matched sklearn within 0.05
RMSE — push that to a full classification dataset). You can explain every parameter because you
implemented it.

## Part B — the full competition pipeline (chapters it tests)

1. **Metric + CV** (ch. 12, 14): implement the exact metric; build a leak-proof,
   distribution-matched CV; run adversarial validation (ch. 13). Confirm CV tracks a holdout.
2. **EDA → features** (ch. 8–10): findings→actions list; groupby aggregations, ratios, datetime,
   missing indicators; leak-safe (K-fold) target encoding; feature selection by
   permutation/null importance (ch. 11), confirmed on CV.
3. **Models** (ch. 7): LightGBM + XGBoost + CatBoost (+ a linear model) under the *same* CV,
   OOF and test predictions saved; light Optuna tuning (ch. 15).
4. **Ensemble** (ch. 16): blend/stack on OOF with airtight discipline; seed averaging (ch. 17).
5. **Metric optimization** (ch. 14): threshold/rounding/calibration on OOF.
6. **Submission**: correctly formatted, finals selected by **CV** (ch. 13), reproducible.
7. **Write-up**: your CV scheme, each experiment's CV vs the baseline, why you chose what you
   chose, and your selection rule.

## Acceptance criteria

- **Part A:** your from-scratch GBM is within a small margin of LightGBM on CV; you can justify
  every parameter.
- **Part B:** the ensemble beats your best single model on CV, which beats the baseline — all on
  the *same* leak-proof CV; CV correlates with a holdout; you produce a correct, reproducible
  submission selected by CV.
- You can explain every step in terms of CV evidence and the leakage discipline — nothing is magic.

## Skills checklist (tick before your next competition)

- [ ] I can build a decision tree and a gradient boosting machine from scratch.
- [ ] I can explain XGBoost vs LightGBM vs CatBoost and tune each.
- [ ] I can engineer groupby/ratio/datetime features and select them with permutation/null
      importance.
- [ ] I can target-encode high-cardinality categoricals without leaking (K-fold + smoothing).
- [ ] I can build a CV that mirrors the test set and prove it with adversarial validation.
- [ ] I can implement any competition metric and optimize predictions to it (threshold/rounding/
      calibration).
- [ ] I can ensemble diverse models by stacking on OOF without leaking.
- [ ] I can run pseudo-labeling and seed averaging safely.
- [ ] I trust my CV over the public leaderboard and select finals by CV.

When every box is ticked, a tabular competition is just disciplined execution — and you've done
the same for LLMs (Bengali capstone). You're a complete competitor on both fronts.
